DT-TacVLA: A Dual-Timescale Tactile-Enhanced Vision–Language–Action Model for Contact-Rich Manipulation

Anonymous Authors
Dual-timescale tactile fusion: slow semantic context + fast reflex modulation

Overview

DT-TacVLA is a dual-timescale tactile fusion framework built on SmolVLA that processes tactile inputs along two complementary pathways. The slow semantic pathway operates at a lower update rate and adaptively fuses tactile image sequences with VLM features through a semantic-guided gating mechanism, producing a reusable task–contact context. The fast reflex pathway is triggered multiple times during the execution of a single action chunk: it encodes the current tactile marker displacements into compact tokens and injects them into the action expert through a Tactile-Marker Adapter, providing real-time contact feedback modulation.

Highlights

  • We propose DT-TacVLA, a dual-timescale tactile-enhanced VLA framework that places tactile image sequences and marker displacements on slow and fast timescales, respectively, inducing a hierarchical coupling between slow-context constraint and fast-feedback modulation.
  • We design a Semantic-Guided Tactile Gating mechanism in the slow semantic pathway that adaptively fuses tactile image-sequence features based on VLM semantic summaries and proprioceptive state, producing a semantically conditioned slow context reusable across multiple fast reflex updates.
  • We propose a Marker-Based Reflex Modulation method that encodes sparse tactile marker displacements into compact token representations and injects them into the action expert as additive residual attention, enabling real-time correction with only modest additional inference cost.
  • DT-TacVLA consistently outperforms vision-only VLA baselines (Diffusion Policy, π0.5, SmolVLA) and ablation variants on three contact-rich real-world manipulation tasks, achieving 91.7% average success rate.

Abstract

Vision–Language–Action (VLA) models exhibit strong semantic understanding and policy generalization, yet their predominantly vision-centric perception struggles to characterize key contact states such as contact establishment, in-hand slip, and normal/shear interactions, which limits their performance in contact-rich manipulation. In this paper, we propose DT-TacVLA (Dual-Timescale Tactile VLA), a dual-timescale tactile fusion framework built on top of SmolVLA that processes tactile inputs along two complementary slow and fast pathways.

The slow semantic pathway operates at a lower update rate and adaptively fuses tactile image sequences with VLM features through a semantic-guided gating mechanism, producing a task–contact context that can be reused across multiple fast reflex updates. The fast reflex pathway is triggered multiple times during the execution of a single action chunk: it encodes the current tactile marker displacements into compact tokens and injects them into the action expert through a Tactile-Marker Adapter under the fixed slow-context conditioning, thereby providing real-time contact feedback modulation of the action chunk.

On three contact-rich real-world manipulation tasks—power plug insertion, USB connector insertion, and test-tube placement—DT-TacVLA consistently outperforms vision-only VLA baselines and its ablation variants in success rate.

Method Overview

DT-TacVLA consists of three core modules:

(a) Slow semantic pathway: The VLM processes vision and language inputs at a low rate and outputs multi-layer hidden states. A tactile sequence encoder extracts temporal features from the recent tactile history. A semantic-guided gating mechanism fuses the semantic summary, the proprioceptive encoding, and the tactile sequence features to produce slow-context conditions, which are written into a reusable K/V cache.

(b) Fast reflex pathway: The action expert reuses the cached slow-context conditions. Meanwhile, a marker extractor obtains low-dimensional tactile markers from the tactile images, encodes them into tactile tokens, and injects them into the action tokens as an additive residual to generate continuous action chunks.

(c) Fast–slow coupling: The slow system updates the context cache at a low rate, while the fast system continuously updates the action chunk under this constraint based on the most recent tactile markers. This hierarchical coupling ensures stable task understanding with responsive contact correction.

Hierarchical modulation of the action expert. The slow context acts as a stable upper-level condition that participates in updating the action tokens; the tactile marker tokens are then injected through the Tactile-Marker Adapter as an additive residual, providing high-rate closed-loop modulation of the action chunk.

Contact-Rich Manipulation Tasks

To evaluate the effectiveness of DT-TacVLA in contact-rich manipulation, we conduct experiments on three real-robot tasks:

  • Power Plug Insertion: Inserting a power plug into a socket with tight tolerance
  • USB Connector Insertion: Inserting a USB connector requiring precise alignment
  • Test Tube Placement: Placing a test tube into a rack with contact-sensitive release

Each task contains two phases—free-space motion and contact interaction—making it possible to test the policy's ability to switch between the vision-dominated non-contact phase and the touch-dominated contact phase and to fuse multimodal information.

Hardware platform: The experimental platform consists of a UR5 robotic arm and an Inspire EG2-4C2 gripper, with one Vitai GF255 vision-based tactile sensor mounted on each fingertip. Visual observations are provided by two RGB-D cameras: an Intel RealSense L515 for the global scene view and an Intel RealSense D435i mounted on the wrist for a close-range end-effector view.

Ablation Study

To verify the contribution of each component, we construct two ablation variants:

  • w/o Gated Fusion: Removes the semantic-guided tactile gating and injects tactile observation directly into the VLM as early-fusion input.
  • w/o Fast Reflex: Removes the fast reflex pathway and retains only the slow semantic pathway.
Variant Power Plug USB Connector Test Tube Average
w/o Gated Fusion 70% 70% 75% 71.7%
w/o Fast Reflex 85% 65% 80% 76.7%
DT-TacVLA (Full) 100% 80% 95% 91.7%

w/o Gated Fusion achieves only 71.7% average success rate (20.0pp lower), indicating that directly injecting tactile observation lacks adaptive regulation with respect to the task phase and proprioceptive state, easily introducing redundant features during free-space motion.

w/o Fast Reflex achieves 76.7% (15.0pp lower), showing that although the slow semantic pathway provides an effective task–contact context, removing the fast reflex pathway leaves the policy unable to quickly respond to the latest shear-field variations and correct contact drift.

Fast–Slow Timescale Analysis

Impact of fast reflex update rate R. The success rate increases as R grows and peaks at R=3. Power plug and USB connector insertion involve tight-tolerance strokes that require multiple fast reflexes to suppress accumulated contact drift, while test-tube placement is less sensitive to the reflex rate.

Conclusion

This paper proposes DT-TacVLA, a dual-timescale tactile-enhanced Vision–Language–Action framework tailored to contact-rich manipulation. Compared with VLA methods that are primarily vision-driven or introduce tactile sensing at a single rate, DT-TacVLA models the task–contact context through the slow semantic pathway and applies instantaneous modulation to the action chunk via the fast reflex pathway using marker displacements, thereby capturing fine contact interactions more effectively.

Real-robot experiments show that DT-TacVLA outperforms vision-only VLA baselines and its ablation variants on power plug insertion, USB connector insertion, and test-tube placement, validating the effectiveness of dual-timescale tactile fusion for high-precision contact manipulation.

Future work will further introduce reinforcement learning to refine the contact policy through interactive trial-and-error on top of the dual-timescale tactile feedback, and to improve the model's robustness under complex contact perturbations, bimanual coordination, and deformable-object manipulation.
