DT-TacVLA: A Dual-Timescale Tactile-Enhanced Vision–Language–Action Model for Contact-Rich Manipulation

Anonymous Authors
Dual-timescale tactile fusion: slow semantic context + fast reflex modulation

Overview

DT-TacVLA is a dual-timescale tactile fusion framework built on top of SmolVLA. Tactile image sequences, together with vision-language-proprioceptive inputs, enter the slow pathway to form a reusable task–contact context; tactile marker displacements serve as high-rate feedback to the fast pathway and instantaneously modulate the action chunks produced by the action expert. The bottom timeline illustrates the update-rate relationship between the slow and fast pathways.

Highlights

  • We propose DT-TacVLA, a dual-timescale tactile-enhanced VLA framework that separates tactile image sequences and marker displacements by their semantic and feedback roles, letting the action expert combine a low-rate VLM-guided context with high-rate marker feedback through hierarchical slow–fast coupling.
  • We design a Semantic-Guided Tactile Gating mechanism that conditions tactile image-sequence fusion on layer-wise VLM semantic summaries and proprioceptive state, producing a reusable slow context for subsequent fast reflex updates.
  • We introduce Marker-Based Reflex Modulation, which encodes sparse tactile marker displacements into compact tactile tokens and injects them through a lightweight adapter, enabling real-time action-chunk correction with low inference overhead.
  • DT-TacVLA consistently outperforms vision-only VLA baselines (Diffusion Policy, π0.5, SmolVLA) on three contact-rich real-world manipulation tasks, achieving 91.7% average success rate.

Abstract

Vision–Language–Action (VLA) models exhibit strong semantic understanding and policy generalization, yet their predominantly vision-centric perception struggles to characterize key contact states such as contact establishment, in-hand slip, and normal and shear contact interactions, which limits their performance in contact-rich manipulation. In this paper, we propose DT-TacVLA (Dual-Timescale Tactile-Enhanced VLA), a dual-timescale tactile fusion framework built on top of SmolVLA that processes tactile inputs along two complementary slow and fast pathways.

The slow semantic pathway operates at a lower update rate and adaptively fuses tactile image sequences with VLM features through a semantic-guided gating mechanism, producing a task–contact context that can be reused across multiple fast reflex updates. The fast reflex pathway is triggered multiple times during the execution of a single action chunk. Under the fixed slow-context conditioning, it encodes the current tactile marker displacements into compact tokens and injects them into the action expert through a Tactile-Marker Adapter, thereby providing real-time contact feedback modulation of the action chunk.

We evaluate DT-TacVLA on three contact-rich real-world manipulation tasks: power plug insertion, USB connector insertion, and test tube placement. The results show that DT-TacVLA consistently outperforms vision-only VLA baselines in success rate.

Method Overview

Given expert demonstration trajectories, DT-TacVLA learns a Vision-Tactile-Language-Action (VTLA) policy that maps visual observation, language instruction, robot proprioceptive state, a recent tactile image sequence, and current tactile marker displacements to a continuous action chunk.

(a) Slow semantic pathway: The VLM processes vision and language inputs at a low rate and outputs multi-layer hidden states aligned with the action expert. TacVideoMAE extracts temporal features from the recent tactile image history, and the semantic-guided gating mechanism fuses the layer-wise semantic summary, proprioceptive encoding, and tactile sequence features to produce slow-context conditions. These conditions are written into a reusable K/V cache and remain fixed across several subsequent fast reflex updates.

(b) Fast reflex pathway: The action expert reuses the cached slow-context conditions. Meanwhile, the marker extractor tracks the tactile marker array in the most recent frame, encodes the marker displacements into compact tactile tokens, and injects them into the action tokens as an additive residual through the Tactile-Marker Adapter.

(c) Fast–slow coupling: The slow pathway determines the task–contact semantics that the action expert should attend to, while the fast pathway applies instantaneous tactile corrections within this context. During inference, the slow K/V cache remains unchanged inside a slow window; only the tactile marker tokens are updated with the current tactile frame.

Hierarchical modulation of the action expert. The layer-wise slow context is projected and cached as K/V, serving as a stable upper-level condition for action-token updates. After attention over this slow context, tactile marker tokens are injected through the Tactile-Marker Adapter as an additive residual, providing high-rate closed-loop modulation of the action chunk.

Contact-Rich Manipulation Tasks

To evaluate the effectiveness of DT-TacVLA in contact-rich manipulation, we conduct experiments on three real-robot tasks:

Each task contains two phases—free-space motion and contact interaction—making it possible to test the policy's ability to switch between the vision-dominated non-contact phase and the touch-dominated contact phase and to fuse multimodal information.

Hardware platform: The experimental platform consists of a UR5 robotic arm and an Inspire EG2-4C2 gripper, with one Vitai GF255 vision-based tactile sensor mounted on each fingertip. Visual observations are provided by two RGB-D cameras: an Intel RealSense L515 for the global scene view and an Intel RealSense D435i mounted on the wrist for a close-range end-effector view.

Experimental protocol: We collect 100 expert demonstration trajectories for each task using a Vive-Tracker-based teleoperation system. Each method is evaluated with 20 real-robot trials per task; before each trial, the object and gripper states are reset and the scene is randomized. A trial is successful only when the insertion or placement is completed and the target remains stable without obvious tipping or jamming.

Implementation details: Global and wrist RGB-D images are captured at 640×480, while the tactile sensor outputs 256×256 tactile images and an 11×11 marker displacement array. The policy uses one tactile sensor stream, the most recent K=4 tactile frames, Nraw=398 TacVideoMAE tokens pooled into Nt=96 tactile tokens, action chunks of length H=50, and R=3 fast reflex updates by default. All methods are trained or fine-tuned for 40,000 optimization steps with batch size 64 on a workstation with two NVIDIA RTX 5090D GPUs.

Diffusion Policy achieves only 36.7% average success because it relies on visual observations and cannot obtain local contact feedback during insertion or placement. The VLA baselines π0.5 and SmolVLA benefit from vision-language pretraining, but they still fail when the contact state is hard to observe externally.

DT-TacVLA achieves the highest success rate on all three tasks. The slow semantic pathway uses tactile image history to identify contact phases and in-hand pose changes, while the fast reflex pathway modulates action generation from the current marker displacements to correct contact drift, insertion jamming, and unstable grasping.

Conclusion

This paper proposes DT-TacVLA, a dual-timescale tactile-enhanced Vision–Language–Action framework tailored to contact-rich manipulation. Compared with VLA methods that are primarily vision-driven or introduce tactile sensing at a single rate, DT-TacVLA models the task–contact context through the slow semantic pathway and applies instantaneous modulation to the action chunk via the fast reflex pathway using marker displacements, thereby capturing fine contact interactions more effectively.

Real-robot experiments show that DT-TacVLA outperforms vision-only VLA baselines on power plug insertion, USB connector insertion, and test tube placement, validating the effectiveness of dual-timescale tactile fusion for high-precision contact manipulation.

Although DT-TacVLA demonstrates strong contact-adaptation capability, the policy still relies primarily on offline demonstration data and cannot fully cover continuous error recovery and long-tailed contact failure states. Future work will introduce reinforcement learning to refine the contact policy through interactive trial-and-error on top of dual-timescale tactile feedback, and will improve robustness under complex contact perturbations, bimanual coordination, and deformable-object manipulation.
